Chapter 1: Dust Growth and Coagulation

Collisional Growth

The first step in planet formation is the sticking of sub-micron dust grains in the protoplanetary disk. Collisions are induced by Brownian motion, turbulence, differential settling, and radial drift. The relative velocity due to Brownian motion is given by:

\[ v_{\text{BM}} = \sqrt{\frac{8kT}{\pi m}} \]

where \(k\) is Boltzmannâ€™s constant, \(T\) is the temperature, and \(m\) is the particle mass.

Role of Turbulence

Turbulence increases collision rates by inducing relative velocities:

\[ v_{\text{turb}} \sim \sqrt{\alpha} \, c_s \left| \text{St}_1 - \text{St}_2 \right| \]

where \(\alpha\) is the Shakura-Sunyaev turbulence parameter, \(c_s\) is the local sound speed, and \(\text{St}\) is the Stokes number.

Fragmentation

At high velocities, instead of sticking, particles fragment. The fragmentation threshold velocity \(v_f\) depends on material properties. For silicates, \(v_f \sim 1 \, \text{m/s}\), while for ices it's \(v_f \sim 10 \, \text{m/s}\).

Growth is halted when \(v_{\text{rel}} > v_f\), leading to the fragmentation barrier.

Sticking and Rebound

Small grains may stick due to van der Waals forces. For larger grains, bouncing becomes likely:

  • Sticking regime: \(v < 0.1 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Bouncing regime: \(0.1 \, \text{m/s} < v < 1 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Fragmentation regime: \(v > 1 \, \text{m/s}\)

This leads to the bouncing barrier, another obstacle to planetesimal formation.

Size Distribution

The evolution of particle size distribution can be modeled using the Smoluchowski coagulation equation:

\[ \frac{\partial n(m)}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^m K(m', m - m') n(m') n(m - m') \, dm' - n(m) \int_0^\infty K(m, m') n(m') \, dm' \]

where \(n(m)\) is the number density of particles of mass \(m\), and \(K(m_1, m_2)\) is the collision kernel.

Observational Evidences

  • Millimeter continuum observations (e.g., ALMA) show grain growth in disks via spectral slopes.
  • Polarization measurements reveal non-spherical and aligned grain growth in some disks.
  • Dust traps inferred from ring/gap structures suggest localized grain growth beyond barriers.
